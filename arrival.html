a.k.a. how my weekend went

I wake up in the dark, to my mother yelling for help. I yell back "Coming!" I stumble to the light switch and put the first shirt I see on backwards while she yells "Hurry!" I give up on pants and stumble to the door. My grandmother is moaning and repeatedly vomiting. I sit at the foot of the bed and hold a tissue box, staring at the wall while my mother holds a bowl of vomit and tries to be reassuring. What happens after that I need to leave out. When the unmentionable thing happens, I feel nothing, and yet I can see in the wall mirror that my body is shaking. A few minutes later I am holding the gate open outside and giving directions to the EMTs, who are so laid back I almost laugh. A few hours later my mother returns from the hospital. She tells me that my grandmother is refusing surgery and so is unlikely to recover. I think back a couple weeks to her birthday, when she told me not to wish her many more. I pause for a moment and say, "Okay." The next day, I feel something like distress on the horizon, but mostly, I just find myself unable to do anything. It's not quite depression--more a subconscious sense of danger in engaging with anything. I fail to even order delivery without feeling a little guilty about it afterwards.

If Adam Wheeler gave it some thought, or if someone were to prompt him with the right questions, he could put words around the fact that his existence doesn't bring him any satisfaction. He would discover, on introspection, that he's nowhere close, actually, to "happy", and that there is something vast and significant missing from his life. But he doesn't give it any thought. There's a void between him and those questions. Objectively, academically, his life is great. As a professional violinist, he does what he loves the most for a living. He has talent, recognition, challenge, variety, applause, a moderate wealth. What is there to question? Why shouldn't he love it? During slower moments, there's a grey worry in the back of his mind. It's there in the minutes right after he wakes up in the morning, before he makes it to the shower; it's there in the dead times backstage when he can't use his phone and there's nothing to do but wait to go on. It perturbs him, from time to time, that he seems to exist in a kind of long shadow, cast by a vast class of thoughts which he is unable to think. But the rest of the time, on a day to day basis, his calendar is as busy as he and his manager can make it. He performs, solo and in orchestras, he records, he composes and teaches. Every week is a different challenge. He keeps busy, and the feeling goes away if he's busy. On the morning of the day that వ arrives, while he is brushing his teeth, a tiny black slug falls out of the corner of his eye into the hotel sink. "Mpfghl?" He scratches that eye, while drooling foam from his toothbrush. He takes a close look at himself in the mirror. Yup: there's another, fatter one growing in there, its tail protruding from his tear duct. "I can do without this," he mutters to himself. He spits, rinses, and then takes a pair of tweezers out of his wash kit. Carefully, he nips the tiny, waving end of the slug, and tugs it out. It's no more painful than extracting a nostril hair. He drops it in the sink with its friend and washes them both away, along with the froth of toothpaste. He stares at the plug hole for a long moment. It's like he's forgetting something. He can't bring it to mind. He shakes his head, and goes to get dressed.

The SCP Antimemetics series is about things that supernaturally prevent knowledge of themselves from spreading. Many of these things are just supernaturally easy to forget, or escape our perception, but the main antagonist, వ, kills you when you think about it, and then prevents people from remembering you ever existed. Like most SCPs, it's easy to make it sound silly if you describe it plainly enough--like, dude, what if someone told you that They Just Lost the Game, and then they fucking died? But it sold me on cosmic horror better than Lovecraft's squeamish racism ever did. When I transitioned, I started pulling apart and scouring my memory to contextualize and re-narrativize my childhood. But "the most merciful thing in the world... is the inability of the mind to correlate its contents." I'd opened the floodgate to all the trauma my mind had been keeping me from. A lot of things conspired over those months to turn me into an agoraphobic, institutionalized suicidal alcoholic, but reflexively gasping at unbidden flashbacks was a factor. Eventually, my brain rewired itself to save me. My memory became foggier. My ADD, which I hadn't recognized up to that point, became significantly worse. The flashbacks would still come, but a few minutes later I'd forget what I was crying about, nip the slugs from my eyes and stare vacantly down the drain. In the Antimemetics series, the Foundation invents a face-sized bacterium that protects you from dangerous thoughts while you wear it as a mask and expunges those thoughts after you've removed it; similarly, I developed a thick lipid bilayer of dissociation, and began fracturing internally to achieve some kind of quasi-plurality. Alex is my bacterium. I've more or less given up on ever working or building a sustainable life for myself again but I'm still here. Adam Wheeler lives in a haze, in a world the reader knows by this point is doomed: వ reaches out to his placid island of ignorance from the black seas of infinity, and ignoring the signs requires immense effort and cognitive dissonance. Immense stretches of his life, good and bad, have been cut away, and he lives in the dissociative shadow of "the thoughts which he is unable to think." Miraculously, the defense mechanisms that have turned him into a depressive husk allow him to survive the arrival of వ. If it's still him that's left, at least.
